Introduction to Psychology

Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes.

Major Perspectives in Psychology:
- Biological: Focuses on how the brain, genetics, and neurotransmitters influence behavior.
- Behavioral: Studies observable behavior. Founded by John B. Watson. B.F. Skinner developed operant conditioning.
- Cognitive: Examines internal mental processes like memory, thinking, and problem-solving.
- Psychodynamic: Based on Sigmund Freud's theories. Emphasizes unconscious motives and childhood experiences.
- Humanistic: Focuses on personal growth and self-actualization. Key figures: Carl Rogers and Abraham Maslow.
- Sociocultural: Examines how social and cultural factors influence behavior.

Classical Conditioning (Ivan Pavlov):
- A learning process where a neutral stimulus becomes associated with a meaningful stimulus.
- Pavlov's experiment: Dogs learned to salivate at the sound of a bell that was paired with food.
- Unconditioned Stimulus (US): Food (naturally causes salivation).
- Unconditioned Response (UR): Salivation in response to food.
- Conditioned Stimulus (CS): Bell (after pairing with food).
- Conditioned Response (CR): Salivation in response to the bell alone.

Operant Conditioning (B.F. Skinner):
- Learning through consequences: reinforcement and punishment.
- Positive reinforcement: Adding something pleasant to increase behavior (e.g., giving a treat).
- Negative reinforcement: Removing something unpleasant to increase behavior (e.g., stopping an alarm).
- Positive punishment: Adding something unpleasant to decrease behavior (e.g., a speeding ticket).
- Negative punishment: Removing something pleasant to decrease behavior (e.g., taking away screen time).

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (bottom to top):
1. Physiological needs: Food, water, shelter, sleep.
2. Safety needs: Security, stability, health.
3. Love and belonging: Friendships, family, romantic relationships.
4. Esteem needs: Self-confidence, achievement, respect from others.
5. Self-actualization: Reaching one's full potential, creativity, purpose.

Memory:
- Sensory memory: Brief storage of sensory information (less than 1 second for visual, 3-4 seconds for auditory).
- Short-term memory: Holds about 7 items (plus or minus 2) for about 20-30 seconds. Also called working memory.
- Long-term memory: Potentially unlimited storage. Divided into explicit (declarative) and implicit (procedural) memory.
- Encoding: Process of getting information into memory.
- Retrieval: Process of getting information out of memory.
- Forgetting: Ebbinghaus forgetting curve shows rapid initial forgetting that levels off over time.
